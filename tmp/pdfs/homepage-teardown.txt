02 — WHAT'S BROKEN
Eight issues, ordered by how much they cost the user. Severity is m y call on impact × reach.
1 The component is called HeroSearch. There is no search.
components/home/hero-search.tsx
The single most common job on a government portal is “find a document / notice / form.” That intent is demoted to a 40px search icon in the
header. The hero instead spends its prime real estate on two poetic couplets.
FIX Put a real, wide search field with 2–3 example queries in the hero. Rename the component to match reality, or make it earn the name.
2 The second headline looks broken, not stylish
“From Village to Value” · text-white/45
Rendering the second couplet at 45%  white opacity reads as disabled or half-loaded, not as intentional emphasis. It also fails contrast against the
green — on a page whose own CSS cites WCAG.
FIX One headline at full contrast, or a clear primary/secondary type scale. Never communicate hierarchy with a contrast failure.
3 Everything is an ALERT, so nothing is
announcement-ticker.tsx
A permanent red “ALERT” badge on an auto-scrolling marquee. Moving text is hard to click, hostile to screen readers and cognition, and the crying-
wolf badge trains users to ignore the one notice that will actually matter.
FIX Drop the marquee. Show the two latest notices as static, dated links. Reserve red for genuinely urgent, time-bound items.
4 Two competing stat displays, back to back
hero STATS array vs. KpiStrip
The hero hardcodes “24 Districts · 3,000+ Members · ₹ 12 Cr+” in the component, while a live CMS-driven KPI strip renders the same class of
numbers one section later. Which is authoritative? They can silently disagree.
FIX One source of truth. Pull hero stats from the same KPI endpoint, or cut the hero strip and let the live KPIs own impact.
5 Leadership headshots outrank citizen tasks
page.tsx — section 3 of 10
Three portraits land above the KPI strip and Quick Access. It’s the familiar govt-portal reflex, but it buries “training, procurement, forms, notices”
beneath a photo wall most visitors did not come for.
FIX Move leadership into About. If it must stay, shrink it to a single slim credit line beneath the task grid.
6 The mobile hero loses its whole right half
right panel — hidden lg:block
The diagonal image panel — the carousel, the captions, all the imagery — is display:none below large screens. On the phones most citizens actually
use, the hero collapses to text on a flat green field.
FIX Give mobile a real hero image, even a single static one above or behind the text. Don’t reserve the visuals for desktop.
7 One template, ten times over
every section on page.tsx
Eyebrow + bold heading + py-14, repeated verbatim, is the entire visual system. There’s no hero-of-the-page moment, no size contrast, no shifts in
layout density — so scanning the page feels like reading a list of identical headers.
FIX Design 2–3 section archetypes (a lead band, a compact utility band, a quiet index band) and assign each section deliberately.
8 Decorative noise dressed as texture
dot grid + concentric circles + ◆  dividers
The hero layers a dot-grid, two decorative circles, and an accent bar; giant Quick-Access numbers render in the near-invisible border color. It’s
generic “govtech template” garnish that adds visual load without adding meaning.
FIX Cut the circles and dot grid. If the big numbers are decorative, make them deliberate; if they’re content, make them legible.
